Introduction

In all modern manufacturing processes, it is essential to observe and validate the condition of components at the output stage. This validation not only ensures the integrity of the physical elements, but also enables the extraction of valuable contextual information about the entire production chain—ranging from raw materials to the influence of external factors such as personnel and machinery.

Within the framework of Industry 4.0, this necessity translates into the demand for enhanced quality control techniques capable of meeting increasingly stringent standards. In this context, and driven by rapid advancements in processing hardware, computer vision has emerged as a powerful and viable solution.

Computer vision refers to a set of techniques for acquiring, processing, analyzing, and interpreting images of the real world in order to generate discrete information that can be processed by digital systems.

Motivation

As manufacturing systems evolve toward full automation, the need for faster, more reliable quality inspection methods becomes increasingly critical. Manual inspections are often subjective and inconsistent, while traditional rule-based vision systems struggle with subtle or previously unseen defects.

Automating defect detection using computer vision addresses these limitations by improving inspection speed, accuracy, and repeatability. This is particularly vital in high-stakes industries such as aerospace, automotive, and pharmaceuticals, where undetected defects can result in costly recalls and safety hazards.

Modern computer vision techniques enable real-time, pixel-level analysis, reducing waste and ensuring compliance with rigorous quality standards. These capabilities align closely with the goals of Industry 4.0, making automated visual inspection a strategic priority for contemporary manufacturing.
