Conclusion

A key finding is the superior performance of simpler models over complex hybrid architectures. Despite integrating YOLOv8 backbones into Keras classifiers, these hybrids did not outperform standalone YOLOv8 or Keras models on cropped images. This underscores the importance of architectural simplicity for better generalization and deployment feasibility in industrial contexts.

The study also highlights the effectiveness of transfer learning and data augmentation, which improved robustness even with limited data. It points toward future exploration of semi-supervised learning to leverage both labeled and unlabeled data, addressing the common scarcity of defect annotations in industry.

Model interpretability was addressed through Grad-CAM, which enhanced transparency by visualizing regions influencing predictions. This builds trust and accountability in automated decisions, especially vital in manufacturing, supporting debugging, regulatory compliance, and user confidence.

Finally, the thesis reflects the ongoing democratization of computer vision. Tools like Roboflow and Google Colab enable development without specialized infrastructure, empowering smaller enterprises. Combined with edge device inference, these advances support scalable, real-time defect detection across diverse production settings.

Future Work

Future work should automate the YOLOv8-to-Keras conversion to improve scalability and reduce errors. Developing an end-to-end pipeline would streamline processing and support real-time deployment in industrial settings.

Efforts should also focus on optimising models for embedded hardware using techniques like quantisation and TensorRT. Incorporating multimodal data (e.g., thermal or acoustic signals) could further improve defect detection beyond standard RGB imagery.
